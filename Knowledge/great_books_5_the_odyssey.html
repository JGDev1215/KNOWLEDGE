Great Books #5: The Odyssey

Introduction: A Family Story

Today we start the Odyssey. And the Odyssey is a sequel to the Iliad and it's a family story. It's a story about a homecoming. There are three main characters. There's Odysseus, who's the main character, and this is why it's called the Odyssey. Then there is Penelope who is his wife, and there's Telemachus who is their son.

And as we discussed when we read the Iliad, Homer is first and foremost concerned about the human condition. What does it mean to be human? What does it mean to live in a world of war, of trauma, of tragedy, and how can we overcome this adversity?

Three Traumatized Individuals

So, the Odyssey is about three individuals in a family and they are all traumatized and they're all heartbroken.

Odysseus is trying to return home from the war and his problem is that he has PTSD, which is post-traumatic stress disorder. And this is very common for soldiers who returned from war. They've seen so much violence, they've seen so much tragedy, they've seen so much senseless death that their souls are shattered. And that is a disease, a problem.

Then you had Penelope who is suffering from depression. And why is she depressed? Because her husband has been away for 20 years and she doesn't know if he's alive or he's dead. He's probably dead, right? It's been 20 years. There's been no sign of him for the past 20 years. But she cannot bring herself to admitting that he's dead. So she's put herself in a very precarious situation. There's dozens of suitors who want her hand in marriage and she wants to say no, but she's afraid that if she does that then she'll be alone for the rest of her life. But she doesn't want to say yes because she refuses to admit that her husband, the love of her life, Odysseus, is dead. In her heart, she feels that he's still alive. But in her head, she cannot justify it. She cannot explain it. It's just a feeling in her.

And so her response, and this is what we call cognitive dissonance, her response is just to shut down. She just spends all her time alone in her room. At first, she's weaving stuff, but then she just sits in her room.

And then there's Telemachus. Telemachus's problem is that he doesn't know if his father is dead or alive but the man is a legend. This is a man who won the Trojan War by coming up with a strategy with the Trojan horse. So he is a legend in this world and everyone sings his praises and he's his son. So he's living in his shadow. And he wants to prove he's better than his father. He wants to become a hero himself, but he's being burdened by the legacy of his father. The other issue is that unless his father is dead, he cannot inherit his father's legacy, right? His property, the reputation cannot be transferred to him. Then there's this mother who's just sitting around the house every single day. If she were to get married, she would leave and then he could be master of his own house. But she's still around. So he's kind of stuck. At the same time, the suitors are waiting for Penelope's answer, which is never going to come. So they're just in the house and they're eating up all his wealth. So he is just seeing his life wither away and there's nothing he can do about it.

He is just depressed as well and he's angry. But you know all these things are the same thing. It's really one of cognitive dissonance. It's really one of depression. It's really one of trauma.

The Soul and the Worldview

So there's another way we can analyze what's going on. And the thing about the soul — the soul is something that the ancients were really concerned about because it was really the most fundamental issue of what it means to be a human being. It's something that we don't talk about today, something that we don't think about today because we live in a world of material science. If we can't see it, it doesn't exist. But for most of human history, humanity has understood the soul as very present and as very real and the most significant thing in our lives.

So the soul that they understood — let's talk about their understanding of the soul, and this is true for actually a lot of cultures. First of all, the soul is a very complex thing. It is almost infinite dimensions. The different layers to the soul and there's really a part of the soul that is you. That's just you, the person. But who you are as a person is ultimately influenced by other influences as well, by other factors as well. So these influences include your family, your culture, the history, and maybe the gods. So again, they're infinite dimensions and these dimensions have an impact on you. And maybe when you die, maybe there's a part of you that lives on. But there's also parts of you that flows back into these different streams of the universe. So it's a very complex thing.

Now the soul — another name for the soul that we use today, if you go to a psychology class, the name for the soul is the worldview. So we have a much more simplistic understanding of our psychology than the ancients. The idea of the worldview is just our understanding of who we are, where we came from, and our place in the universe. And we believe it comes from our memories and our experiences.

And the worldview is important because it allows us to know ourselves. This is important for things such as planning — only if you know who you are can you actually plan ahead because then you know what you want to do, what you want to accomplish. It allows for empathy, building relationships with others. And it allows for judgment — basically, what's good? What's bad? What do you like? What don't you like?

So, the worldview and the soul are fundamental to who we are. Now, in psychology, what they will teach you is that trauma splinters the worldview. There's something about your experience that is fundamental to your identity but because it is so painful you forget about it or you refuse to see it and this causes a rupture in your identity, in your coherent sense of the world, which destroys your worldview.

And this leads to depression. What is depression? Depression is the inability to act and to think. You don't know what you want to accomplish. You don't trust yourself. You don't know what you like. And so what are the symptoms of depression? You sleep a lot. You find no pleasure in anything. You feel no emotion. You feel sort of numbness. And that's why people tend to take drugs or they just sleep all the time or whatever. Because you are unable to act.

And that's exactly what's happening to Odysseus, Penelope, and Telemachus. There's a trauma in their lives. And they just believe this as well — there could be a trauma that splits the soul. It could be a demon. It could be a really painful incident. It could be anything. But it splits your soul.

And if you are to recover, then you have to repair your soul. And that's a fundamental conflict and dilemma within the Odyssey. You have three individuals, Odysseus, Penelope, Telemachus. Their soul has been splintered. And so they're on a journey, their own odyssey to repair their soul. And the answer of course is each other. The love for each other. That's what's going to save the world. The love of family — and that is the fundamental message of the Odyssey. And that's why it's such a powerful read.

Background: Why Odysseus Goes to Troy

So let me give you some background as to the plot. So, as you know, the Odyssey begins in medias res — in the middle of things — which is to say that a lot of things have already taken place and it happens just in the middle of the plot. In the beginning, Odysseus has been away for 20 years and he's stuck on an island with Calypso. Penelope is depressed and Telemachus is angry. Athena comes and tells Telemachus, "Come with me. I will help you find your father."

So the story starts with the Trojan War, of course. Remember, Helen has been taken to Troy. She falls in love with Paris because that's what Aphrodite promised Paris as a prize. And now her husband, the king of Sparta, Menelaus, and his brother Agamemnon, the king of kings, are like, "We're going to raise an army and we're going to get back Helen." And this is prophesized. This is to be the greatest war in human history. It will make mortals into gods. That's why Achilles enters this war because this is the opportunity for him to prove that he is the greatest warrior of all in all of human history and that's why he goes to Troy.

Odysseus is also supposed to go to this war. But Odysseus, he's different from Achilles because Odysseus loves his wife Penelope and he has a new son, six months old, Telemachus. So he's conflicted and he really doesn't want to go to war. But the Greeks need him because the gods have told the Greeks that it is really Odysseus who will win the war because he's the one who will come up with the Trojan horse, the stratagem that will win this war. So you need this guy.

So Odysseus doesn't want to go but the Greeks insist. So the Greeks send two messengers to Ithaca where Odysseus lives. Now what he does is he pretends to be mad. So he dresses up as a beggar. He's a king but he dresses up really badly and he's going around the farmland and throwing salt everywhere he goes. He looks crazy. And you throw salt in the land to kill crops. So he looks kind of crazy, but the Greeks understand that this is a man of disguise. He's a man of stratagems. He's very clever. So it's probably a trick. So what they do is they take his six-month-old son Telemachus and put him in front of Odysseus. Odysseus is plowing the field and salting the fields. And basically the test is this: if you're really mad, you're going to run over your son Telemachus and kill him. If you're not mad, if you're not really crazy, then you're going to stop and go somewhere else and save your son. So let's see if you're really crazy.

And so Odysseus of course turns away and saves his son and they say, "Ha! You see, you were tricking us all along. Come with us to Troy." And so Odysseus has no choice but to go.

So this is why Odysseus goes. He's forced to go. You think about it, the story doesn't really make any sense, right? And that's the greatness of Homer because he's telling us the complexity of human psychology. Odysseus is really really smart. He thinks ahead. He should know that if he were to have the stratagem, the Greeks would see through it really quickly. So what he should be doing is hiding from the Greeks, or maybe just moving away. If you really really don't want to go to this war, you just run away and take your family with you. Or you know what, just kill these guys because you're crazy, right?

So what this tells us is that yes, Odysseus really loves his family and he wants to be with his family because he knows that if he goes to Troy, he'll be gone for 20 years. That's 20 years that he won't be able to see his son Telemachus grow up. But at the same time, this is the greatest war in human history. Achilles is going. Agamemnon is going. Does Odysseus really want to be left out? Does he really want to be the one guy who is like a coward who is like, "You know what, I'm just going to stay home." The Greeks will just laugh at him for the rest of his life. He doesn't want that. So, it's a conflict. So, what he does is he comes up with a strategy that allows the Greeks to see through it really quickly and force him to go. So, he doesn't have to bear any responsibility for what he does. And that is a complexity of the human soul. That is a complexity of our worldview.

Odysseus's Worldview at Troy

So he goes to Troy and he has to explain to himself why he's there. And he knows he's going to be away from home for 20 years, 10 years in Troy, 10 years being lost at sea. So his worldview is this:

Why am I here in Troy? Well, the first reason is justice. This is a just war because the Trojans stole Helen and therefore the Greeks must avenge the honor of men.

Second is family. Helen should be with her children. Helen should be with her family in Sparta. We have to return her to Sparta. How would I feel if Penelope was stolen from me? I would want everyone to go fight with me to get her back as well. It's about family.

Now, third reason is legacy. Yes, he will not see his son Telemachus for 20 years. Therefore, he has to build him a legacy that will carry him for the rest of his life. If he is in Troy and he wins this great war and he becomes this great hero, then Telemachus himself will be famous as a son of Odysseus. People will respect Telemachus. He wants to build a legacy for his son. He wants to tell his son this is how you should live your life. You should live a life of honor, of justice, of truth.

So these are the three reasons why he goes to Troy. And we have to remember that this is all to justify his conflict. There's a conflict. There's a rupture in his soul. He needs to mend it by explaining why he's doing what he's doing. Because again, he's abandoning his family for 20 years.

The Worldview Shatters

So what's going to happen is this — this is the worldview that Odysseus has when he enters Troy. The problem starts when they win the war. Because when they win the war, Odysseus is forced to see that all these three things were wrong.

He will discover that this war was not about justice. It was just about revenge and murder. It is not about family. It's about destroying families because when you win this war, you enslave the women and kill the husbands. That's destroying families. And it's not about legacy because you've destroyed the Trojan civilization for no particular reason because Helen doesn't even want to go back to Sparta.

And this causes cognitive dissonance and this is what causes his PTSD, or trauma. And so the Odyssey is his journey to repair his soul so that he can go home.

But that's the Iliad. And again what will happen is that Odysseus will win this war and he will win eternal glory for himself with the stratagem of the Trojan horse. The Source of Odysseus's Trauma

So now we know what happened. For 10 years, Odysseus and the Greeks were trying to defeat the Trojans and they could not do it because the Trojans were behind a walled city. So Odysseus prayed and prayed to Athena for inspiration and then one day he had it. The Trojan horse. So he sneaks in. So he gets into the underbelly of the Trojan horse along with other soldiers. And then they get into the city and they're really scared, they're really excited. Then they're able at night to leave the Trojan horse and then open the gates of Troy and they stream in and they start to conquer the city.

And then Odysseus, you know, he is killing, because it's war and he's full of adrenaline. But then something catches his eye. He strikes down a soldier and then a woman comes crying. And she's traumatized at the sight of the death of her husband.

And Odysseus is traumatized by this woman being traumatized because he came to Troy to save the woman, right? He sees Penelope in this woman. He says, "I came all this way to make sure that Helen could be safe with her husband." And now he just recognized that he just killed someone's husband. But not only that, the Greeks come and they take her off to be enslaved.

So he, by winning this war, has destroyed every single family in Troy. He's caused the suffering of thousands and thousands of women and children.

And that is what he sees for the first time. For the longest time he believed that he was here to fight for justice, to save families, to leave a legacy for his son Telemachus. And now he recognizes: no, this is not a just war. I'm destroying a civilization. This is not a war about family. This is a war about destroying families. And what legacy am I leaving my son? How would my son feel if he knows how I destroyed families like ours?

And this causes trauma in him. It splits his soul and it drives him crazy basically. And that's the conflict, the central conflict in the Odyssey.

Having done so much evil, how can you go home? How can you repair yourself? How can you mend your soul and repair your worldview so that you can live for your family?
