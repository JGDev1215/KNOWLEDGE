Part I · Of the Prophecies of Daniel
Chapter I · Introduction
Of the Compilers of the Books of the Old and New Testament
Concerning Daniel's authority and the nature of prophecy
On the Authority of Daniel
Daniel was in the greatest credit and esteem among the Jews, until they began to reject Jesus. Josephus says that he was one of the greatest of the Prophets, for he did not only prophesy of future things, as did the other Prophets, but he also determined the time of their accomplishment. And his prophecies were so plain and circumstantial, that some of the Heathen took him rather for an Historian than a Prophet.
Newton draws upon Josephus to establish Daniel's singular prophetic authority before proceeding to interpret the visions.
On the Nature of Prophetic Writing
The authority of the Prophets is divine, and comprehends the sum of religion; reckoning Moses and the Prophets, Christ and his Apostles, as so many witnesses of the truth. To reject their testimony is to reject the Christian religion. And therefore the question is not whether the prophecies are genuine, but whether they are rightly interpreted.
Newton frames his inquiry as one of proper interpretation rather than of authentication.
On the Gradual Opening of Prophecy
The folly of interpreters has been to foretell times and things by this Prophecy, as if God designed to make them Prophets. By this rashness they have not only exposed themselves, but brought the Prophecy also into contempt. The design of God was much otherwise. He gave this and the Prophecies of the Old Testament, not to gratify men's curiosities by enabling them to foreknow things; but that after they were fulfilled, they might be interpreted by the event, and his own Providence, not the Interpreters, be then manifested thereby to the world.
Newton sets forth his foundational interpretive principle: prophecy is meant to be understood after fulfilment, not to satisfy curiosity beforehand.
On Reason and Study
For it is the perfection of God's works that they are all done with the greatest simplicity. He is the God of order and not of confusion. And therefore as they that would understand the frame of the world, must endeavour to reduce their knowledge to all possible simplicity, so it must be in seeking to understand these Prophecies. He who searcheth the Scriptures must search them with all his might, and with a sincere desire to understand them.
Chapter II
Of the Prophetic Language
The system of figurative speech employed in Daniel's visions
Beasts as Kingdoms
The Prophets describe nations and kingdoms under the figures of Beasts and Birds, and therefore the natures and qualities of those Beasts and Birds are to be considered in interpreting the prophecy. As a Lion, Bear, Leopard, and the fourth Beast dreadful and terrible — these are the figures by which the four great empires of the world are set forth.
This is Newton's master key: prophetic language is a consistent figurative system, not arbitrary imagery.
Horns as Kings
The parts of the world signified in Prophecy are Horns for Kings and Kingdoms, Heads for supreme Governors and supreme Governments, Feet for the lowest ranks of men. A Mountain signifies the Nation or Kingdom which it represents; a Rock, the same in a harder and more permanent sense. Thus the stone which smote the image, and became a great mountain, is the kingdom of God set up in the latter times.
Newton catalogues the standard figurative equivalences he will apply throughout his interpretation.
The Consistency of the Prophetic Tongue
The whole system of the Prophetic language is to be understood before the Prophecies themselves can be understood. The language was invented as a common language to the Prophets who all spoke alike, one from another, understanding one another's figurative expressions. The same figure is used by the same signification through the whole Prophecy, and it is to be presumed that it continues to hold the same signification until there be cause to the contrary.
Celestial Bodies
The Sun signifies the whole species and body of any great people or empire. The Moon signifies the body of the common people considered as the wife of some supreme power. The Stars signify the subordinate Princes and great men, or Bishops and Rulers of the People. And therefore when the Dragon drew a third part of the stars and cast them to the earth, this signified a third part of the rulers and great ones of the empire falling from their heights.
Chapter III
Of the Vision of the Image composed of Four Metals
Nebuchadnezzar's dream and the four successive world-empires
The Dream Described
Thou, O King, sawest, and behold a great Image. This great Image, whose brightness was excellent, stood before thee; and the form thereof was terrible. This Image's head was of fine gold, his breast and his arms of silver, his belly and his thighs of brass, his legs of iron, his feet part of iron and part of clay. The Image represents the succession of four great empires which were to govern the world until the establishment of the Kingdom of God.
Newton cites the scriptural text directly before proceeding to interpret the successive metals.
The Head of Gold — Babylon
Babylon

The head of gold is the kingdom of Nebuchadnezzar — the empire of the Babylonians and Chaldeans. It was the first of the four great empires, and was represented by gold as the most noble of metals, for this was the mightiest of the four, and in Nebuchadnezzar its glory reached its height. Thou art this head of gold, said the Prophet to the King.
The Breast of Silver — Medes and Persians
Persia

After thee shall arise another kingdom inferior to thee — the empire of the Medes and Persians, represented by silver. The Medes and Persians overthrew Babylon, and ruled a greater extent of territory, yet were considered inferior in dignity, being a compound of two nations united under Cyrus. The double arms of silver represent the two peoples — the Medes and the Persians — who together constituted this empire.
The Belly of Brass — Greece
Greece

The third kingdom of brass, which shall bear rule over all the earth, is the Empire of the Greeks, beginning with Alexander the Great. Alexander's swift conquests from Macedonia to India, overturning Persia in a few campaigns, are the type of the rapid Leopard with four wings. The belly and thighs of brass suggest the breadth of the Macedonian dominion.
The Legs of Iron — Rome
Rome

The fourth kingdom shall be strong as iron: forasmuch as iron breaketh in pieces and subdueth all things. This is the Roman Empire, which succeeded the Greek and crushed all before it. The two legs of iron indicate the division of the Roman Empire into the Eastern and Western Empires. As iron is stronger than any previous metal, so Rome in its power exceeded the other three empires.
Newton's interpretation is firm and consistent with the traditional patristic reading that he adopts as a secure foundation.
The Stone cut without Hands
Thou sawest till that a stone was cut out without hands, which smote the image upon his feet that were of iron and clay, and brake them to pieces. This stone is the Kingdom of God, which in the last times shall destroy all earthly kingdoms and fill the whole earth. It is cut without hands because it is not founded by human power, but by divine will. That it smites the image upon the feet signifies that the final kingdoms — the fragmented successors of Rome — are those against which the divine kingdom is ultimately set.
Chapter IV
Of the Vision of the Four Beasts
Daniel's night vision of four beasts rising from the sea
The Vision Introduced
The four Beasts in Daniel's vision are the same four empires as the metals of Nebuchadnezzar's Image, only represented by a different figure. The Image sets forth their outward glory and political succession; the Beasts set forth their inward nature. Four great beasts came up from the sea, diverse one from another.
The First Beast — The Lion
Babylon

The first was like a Lion, and had Eagle's wings. This is the Empire of the Babylonians. The Lion is the king of beasts, and the Eagle the king of birds, to signify the high estate of the Babylonian empire. I beheld till the wings thereof were plucked, and it was lifted up from the earth, and made stand upon the feet as a man, and a man's heart was given to it — a reference, Newton interprets, to Nebuchadnezzar's humbling and subsequent restoration.
The Second Beast — The Bear
Persia

The second beast like a Bear raised up on one side, and it had three ribs in the mouth of it between the teeth of it, and they said thus unto it: Arise, devour much flesh. The Bear raised on one side signifies the Persians being more powerful than the Medes, the one side rising above the other. The three ribs are the three kingdoms it devoured — Lydia, Babylon, and Egypt — thus confirming the Bear as the Medo-Persian empire.
The Third Beast — The Leopard
Greece

The third beast like a Leopard, which had upon the back of it four wings of a fowl; the beast had also four heads, and dominion was given to it. The Leopard with four wings represents the swiftness of Alexander's conquests. The four heads represent the four kingdoms into which Alexander's empire was divided after his death — those of his four principal successors: Cassander over Macedon, Lysimachus over Thrace and Asia Minor, Seleucus over Syria and the East, and Ptolemy over Egypt.
The Fourth Beast — Rome
Rome

The fourth beast, dreadful and terrible, and strong exceedingly; and it had great iron teeth: it devoured and brake in pieces, and stamped the residue with the feet of it: and it was diverse from all the beasts that were before it; and it had ten horns. This is the Roman Empire. It is diverse from the other beasts because it was not a monarchy of one nation, but rather grew by slow degrees through conquest and constitution, unlike the swift succession of the prior empires. Its great iron teeth devour and break in pieces — as iron is stronger than brass, silver, or gold, so Rome was stronger than its predecessors.
Newton observes that Rome is the only beast not named by type — it has no natural equivalent, being unique and unprecedented among world powers.
The Ancient of Days
The vision closes with the Ancient of Days sitting in judgment, with the books opened and the fourth beast slain. I saw in the night visions, and behold, one like the Son of Man came with the clouds of heaven, and came to the Ancient of Days, and they brought him near before him. And there was given him dominion, and glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages should serve him. This Newton understands as the establishment of the everlasting Kingdom of God, which succeeds and supersedes all the empires of the vision.
Chapter V
Of the Kingdoms represented by the Feet of Iron and Clay
The fragmented successor kingdoms of Rome
Iron Mixed with Clay
And whereas thou sawest iron mixed with miry clay, they shall mingle themselves with the seed of men: but they shall not cleave one to another, even as iron is not mixed with clay. By the feet and toes partly of potters' clay and partly of iron, the kingdom shall be divided. The iron and clay that will not cohere represents kingdoms that shall attempt alliance through marriage and treaty, but shall not unite into a single empire as the legs of iron had been. They shall remain perpetually divided.
Newton uses this passage to establish that the divided state of Europe — many kingdoms never fully reuniting into a single Roman Empire — is itself the fulfilment of prophecy.
On the Impossibility of Reunion
This Prophecy, if I am not mistaken, is to be understood of the western empire of the Romans after it became broken into many kingdoms. The kingdoms shall attempt union by the marriages of their royal families, but shall not cleave together. The history of Europe from the fall of Rome is the history of this very prophecy — perpetual attempted union, perpetual failure to achieve it. The iron of Roman constitution persists in the laws and customs of these nations, but the clay of their diverse original peoples prevents any permanent amalgam.
Chapter VI
Of the Ten Kingdoms represented by the Ten Horns
The division of the Western Roman Empire
The Ten Horns Interpreted
The ten horns of the fourth beast are ten kings that shall arise out of the Roman empire. The fourth beast shall be the fourth kingdom upon earth, which shall be diverse from all kingdoms, and shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it in pieces. And the ten horns out of this kingdom are ten kings that shall arise. By the gradual breaking up of the Western Roman Empire, Newton identifies the ten kingdoms as those nations which carved up the former Roman territories.
The Kingdoms Named
Newton identifies these ten kingdoms as the Visigoths in Spain, the Ostrogoths in Italy, the Vandals in Africa, the Lombards in Italy after the Ostrogoths, the Burgundians, the Franks, the Britons, the Huns, the Alemanni, and the Saxons in Britain. These kingdoms arose in the western parts of the empire in the fourth, fifth, and sixth centuries, and divided the western empire among themselves.
Newton gives the kingdoms as an interpretive enumeration, acknowledging that scholars differ on the precise identification of all ten.
On the Symbolic Number Ten
Whether the number ten be exact, or only approximate, Newton argues that the prophetic vision intends to signify the complete and total fragmentation of the Roman Empire's western body. The exact enumeration of ten horns is not material, if the general meaning be held — that the Roman Empire in the west should be divided into a number of independent sovereign kingdoms. The prophetic number is fulfilled in the general fact of division.
Chapter VII
Of the Eleventh Horn of Daniel's Fourth Beast
The rise of a new power among the ten kingdoms
The Little Horn
I considered the horns, and behold, there came up among them another little horn, before whom there were three of the first horns plucked up by the roots: and behold, in this horn were eyes like the eyes of man, and a mouth speaking great things. This horn is diverse from the others, for it is a religious power as well as a political one — the eyes of a man signify human wisdom and policy; the mouth speaking great things signifies arrogance and claims to divine authority.
Three Horns Plucked Up
The three horns plucked up to make room for the little horn are three of the ten kingdoms of the fragmented Roman Empire which were overthrown. The little horn, diverse from the ten horns, which came up among them, had eyes like the eyes of man, and a mouth speaking great things, whose look was more stout than his fellows. Newton interprets this as a power which arose on the ruins of three kingdoms, asserting both temporal and spiritual supremacy — combining kingly and priestly authority in a single seat.
Newton is careful to let the text describe the power before drawing conclusions, presenting each feature of the vision as a distinct prophetic token.
A Time, Times, and Half a Time
And he shall speak great words against the Most High, and shall wear out the saints of the Most High, and think to change times and laws: and they shall be given into his hand until a time and times and the dividing of time. Newton takes the prophetic formula of a time, and times, and half a time — that is, one year, two years, and half a year, totalling three and a half years. In prophetic reckoning, a prophetic day stands for a solar year, giving 1260 prophetic years as the duration of this power's dominion.
Chapters VIII–IX
Of the Vision of the Ram and the He-Goat
The kingdoms of Persia and Greece
The Ram with Two Horns
I saw in my vision, and I was by the river of Ulai. Then I lifted up mine eyes, and saw, and behold, there stood before the river a Ram which had two horns: and the two horns were high; but one was higher than the other, and the higher came up last. The Ram with the two horns, of which one is higher, is the empire of the Medes and Persians — the one horn being the Medes and the other the Persians. The higher horn which came up last is the Persians, who became dominant in the later history of the empire.
The He-Goat from the West
Greece

And as I was considering, behold, a He-Goat came from the west on the face of the whole earth, and touched not the ground: and the goat had a notable horn between his eyes. And he came to the ram that had two horns, and ran unto him in the fury of his power. The He-Goat coming from the west with such speed that it touches not the ground is Alexander the Great, whose campaigns moved with unprecedented swiftness. The notable horn between his eyes is the first King — Alexander himself.
The Great Horn Broken and Four Others
And the He-Goat waxed very great: and when he was strong, the great horn was broken; and for it came up four notable ones toward the four winds of heaven. At the height of his power Alexander died, and his great empire was divided among four of his generals — the four notable horns coming up in place of the one great horn. These four horns toward the four winds of heaven are the four kingdoms of the successors: Macedon, Thrace, Syria, and Egypt, each occupying a different quarter of Alexander's empire.
The Angel's Interpretation to Daniel
The Angel Gabriel interprets the vision directly for Daniel: The Ram which thou sawest having two horns are the kings of Media and Persia. And the rough goat is the king of Grecia: and the great horn that is between his eyes is the first king. Now that being broken, whereas four stood up for it, four kingdoms shall stand up out of the nation, but not in his power. Newton observes that this angelic interpretation is itself a model of how to read prophetic figures, and that the rule of correspondence — beast = kingdom, horn = king — is divinely ordained rather than humanly invented.
Chapter X
Of the Prophecy of the Seventy Weeks
The most exact of Daniel's prophetic chronologies
The Prophecy Stated
Know therefore and understand, that from the going forth of the commandment to restore and to build Jerusalem unto the Messiah the Prince shall be seven weeks, and threescore and two weeks: the street shall be built again, and the wall, even in troublous times. The Seventy Weeks is, Newton holds, the most precise and determinate of all Daniel's prophecies. Its fulfilment in exact years is a supreme evidence both of Daniel's divine authority and of the providential ordering of history.
Calculation of the Weeks
Newton applies the prophetic principle that each day in a prophecy of weeks signifies a year. The Seventy Weeks therefore signify 490 years. Of these: the first seven weeks (49 years) are allotted to the rebuilding of Jerusalem in troublous times, under Nehemiah and Ezra. The following threescore and two weeks (434 years) span from the completion of the city to the coming of the Messiah Prince. This makes 483 years in total to the cutting off of the Messiah. The remaining one week concerns the confirmation of the covenant.
The Commencement of the Seventy Weeks
The commandment to restore and build Jerusalem Newton identifies with the decree of Artaxerxes Longimanus in the seventh year of his reign, when he empowered Ezra to restore the Jewish polity. From this date, Newton reckons forward through the 483 years to arrive at the time of the ministry and death of Christ — a remarkable and, in Newton's judgment, exact agreement between the prophetic number and the historical record.
Newton's arithmetical argument for the precise fulfilment of the Seventy Weeks forms one of the central pillars of his work, combining historical scholarship with prophetic interpretation.
The Cutting Off of the Messiah
And after threescore and two weeks shall Messiah be cut off, but not for himself. Newton reads this as the plainest of all prophecies concerning Christ. That the Messiah should be cut off — put to death — after the appointed 69 weeks, and that this is not for his own fault but for the sins of others, is a straightforward prediction of the Crucifixion. The Passion of Christ therefore does not interrupt or overthrow the scheme of Daniel — it is the very event toward which the whole prophetic chronology had been pointing from the beginning.
Chapter XI
Of the Times of the Birth and Passion of Christ
Reconciling Daniel's chronology with the historical record
On Determining the Year
The years of the Birth and Passion of Christ have been much disputed among Chronologers. Newton undertakes to determine them from the text of Daniel and from the known synchronisms of Roman and Jewish history. The main difficulty consists in determining the regnal years of the Persian kings, and the exact year of the decree of Artaxerxes, from which the Seventy Weeks are to be numbered.
The Passover and the Passion
Newton examines the astronomical evidence for the year of the Crucifixion. The Passover full moon on the 14th of Nisan, which was the day of the Passion, can be placed astronomically in one of several years within the probable range. Newton, from a combination of chronological, astronomical, and historical argument, concludes that the Passion of our Saviour fell upon Friday the 3rd of April in the year of Christ 33 — reckoned by the Julian Calendar — and that this agrees well with the 483 years counted from the decree of Artaxerxes.
Newton applies the same mathematical rigour to prophetic chronology that he applied to natural philosophy — treating the problem as a precise calculation from data.
Chapter XII
Of the Prophecy of the Scripture of Truth
The great prophecy of the kings of the north and south
The Scope of the Scripture of Truth
The eleventh chapter of Daniel is called by the Angel the Scripture of Truth, and it is the longest and most detailed prophecy in the book. It is a history — written in the form of prophecy — of the wars between the King of the North and the King of the South, from the time of the Persian empire down through the Greek successors and on into the Roman period. Newton observes that the precision of its historical correspondence is such that some ancient critics refused to believe it could be genuine prophecy, attributing it to an historian writing after the fact.
Newton takes this very precision as proof of its divine origin: what seems too accurate to be prophecy is, for that very reason, evidence of God's omniscience.
The King of the North and South
The King of the South throughout the earlier sections of this prophecy is the Ptolemaic king of Egypt. The King of the North is the Seleucid king of Syria. The wars of these two dynasties — the Ptolemies and the Seleucids — fill the early portions of the prophecy in exact historical sequence. Newton traces each verse of the prophecy to its specific historical fulfilment, showing that the text anticipates events which did not occur until decades or centuries after Daniel's time.
